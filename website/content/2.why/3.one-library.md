# One Library, Not Five

A normal TypeScript runtime stack is a small zoo. A validator from over here. A JSON serializer that handles `Date` from over there. A binary codec when payload size matters. A mock-data generator for tests. Something for reflection if you're feeling brave.

Five libraries, five authors, five mental models of what your type *means* — and you, in the middle, gluing them together and praying they agree.

They don't, quite. They can't.

## One type graph to rule them all

When ts-run-types reads your type, it builds one internal model of it — a type graph — and **everything** is generated from that same graph. The validator, the JSON encoder and decoder, the binary codec, the mock generator, the reflection id. All of it, one source.

That graph is shared and deduplicated, hard:

- Two types with the same shape collapse to **one** entry — by pointer identity *and* by structure.
- A node exists exactly **once** across your whole app, no matter how many places reach for it.
- You only ship the functions you actually call. Caches are demand-driven and every entry is its own module, so a file that only reflects an id ships zero validation code. Bundlers tree-shake the rest to the bone.

Five separate libraries can't do this. Each parses your types (or your schema) on its own, in its own format, in its own bundle. Five copies of the same understanding, none of them talking.

## Consistency you can't bolt on

Here's the failure mode nobody warns you about. Your validator says the payload is a valid `User`. Your serializer, parsing the *same* type through a *different* library, encodes a field the validator never checked — or drops one it did. Now your "validated" data and your "serialized" data disagree about what a `User` is, and you find out in production at 2am.

That happens because two libraries made two independent guesses about the same type. Optionality, nullability, how a discriminated union narrows, what an `unknown` even is — pick five libs and you'll get a small spread of opinions.

> When the validator and the serializer are generated from the same type graph, they cannot disagree about your type. There's only one answer to disagree with.

That's the quiet feature: consistency that isn't a config option you remember to set. It's structural. Validate, encode to JSON, ship as binary, mock it for a test — every one of those is the *same* understanding of `T`, just specialized into a different function.

And it's less drift, all the way down. One library to version-bump. One model to learn. One set of [type-support](/reference/type-support) rules, not five Venn diagrams you keep in your head.

## What an integrated library buys the ecosystem

Tools that share a type graph compound. A new capability — say, a different serialization format — drops in as another consumer of the *same* graph and instantly understands every type the rest already do. No "does the new lib support unions the same way?" archaeology.

It also means improvements land once and lift everything. Sharpen how the type graph models a tricky type and the validator, the JSON codec, the binary codec, the mocker and reflection all get sharper together — because they're not five things, they're five views of one thing.

> Five libraries can never agree on what your type means. One library only has to mean it once.

That's the whole pitch for one box instead of five. Now the honest part — [what we're *not*](/why/what-were-not), and where this approach stops.
